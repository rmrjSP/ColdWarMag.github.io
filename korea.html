Korean War

The Korean peninsula had been part of the Japanese Empire, and after WWII it fell to the Americans and the Soviets to decide what should be done. In August 1945, the State Department divided the Korean peninsula in half along the 38th parallel. The Russians occupied the area north of the line and the U.S. occupied the area to its south. The Korean War was the first military action of the Cold War. It was fought between North Korea and South Korea. It began on June 25th, 1950 when around 75,000 soldiers from the North Korean People’s army crossed the 38th Parallel. The 38th parallel was the boundary between the Soviet-backed Democratic People’s Republic of Korea in the North and the US backed Republic of Korea in the South. Many feared that it was the first step in a communist campaign to take over the world. The fight on the Korean peninsula was a symbol of the global struggle between east and west in the Cold War. At first the Korean war was a defensive one to rid South Korea of cummunists. However, after being bested by the North Korean army, the U.S. decided to go on the offensive. It was a war to “liberate” the North from communists. At first the strategy was succeeding, and they managed to push back the North Koreans to their side of the 38th parallel. The American troops pushed north toward the Yalu River, which was the border between North Korea and Communist China. The Chinese began to worry about protecting themselves from what they called “armed aggression against Chinese territory.” The Chinese sent troops to North Korea and told the U.S. to keep away from the Yalu boundary unless it wanted full-scale war. President Truman and his advisors did not want war with China and were sure that such a war would lead to Soviet aggression in Europe. In March 1951, General Douglas MacArthur sent a letter to a House Republican leader, who shared MacArthur’s support for declaring war on China, to leak the letter to the press. President Truman fired General MacArthur for insubordination on April 11. President Truman and his new military commanders started peace talks at Panmunjom in July 1951. The fighting continued while the peace talks slowed. The Chinese and North Koreans agreed that the prisoners of war should be forcefully “repatriated” while the U.S. disagreed. After more than two years of negotiations, an armistice was signed on July 27, 1953. The agreement allowed the POWs to stay where they liked and drew a new boundary near the 38th parallel that gave South Korea an additional 1,500 square miles of territory. A 2-mile-wide “demilitarized zone” was created and still exists today. In the Korean War, nearly 5 million people died. More than half of these were civilians and almost 40,000 Americans died in action in Korea. They are remembered today at the Korean War Veterans Memorial near the Lincoln Memorial on the National Mall in Washington, D.C. The memorial is a series of 19 steel statues of servicemen. There is also a memorial in Fullerton, California, and it was the first on the West Coast to include names of the more than 30,000 Americans who died during the war.
