Nikita Khruschev

Nikita Khrushchev, full name Nikita Sergeyevich Khrushchev, was born April 17, 1894 in Kalinovka, Russia. Khrushchev was the son of a coal miner and his grandfather had been a serf who served in the tsarist army. He and his family went to Yuzovka, a mining center in the Donets Basin, where he worked as a pipe fitter at the age of 15. His factory employment excused him from being conscripted into the tsarist army during WWI. Khrushchev had been very active in workers’ organizations, and in 1918, he became a member of the Russian Communist Party. He joined the Red Army in January 1919, where he served as a junior political commissar. His wife, Galina, died during a famine soon after he had been demobilized. In 1922 Khrushchev secured admission to a new Soviet workers’ school in Yuzovka, where he received a secondary education along with additional party instruction. He became a student political leader and was appointed secretary of the Communist Party Committee at the school. In 1924, he married his second wife, Nina Petrovna, a schoolteacher. In 1925 Khrushchev went into full-time party work as party secretary of the Petrovsko-Mariinsk district of Yuzovka. He soon came to the notice of Joseph Stalin’s close associate, Lazar M. Kaganovich, secretary general of the Ukrainian Party’s Central Committee, who asked Khrushchev to accompany him as a non-voting delegate to the 14th Party Congress in Moscow. In 1929, he received permission to go to Moscow to study metallurgy at the Stalin Industrial Academy. He was appointed secretary of the academy’s Party Committee. In 1931 he went back to full-time party work in Moscow and by 1933 he had become second secretary of the Moscow Regional Committee. During the early 1930s Khrushchev supervised the completion of the Moscow subway, for which we received the Order of Lenin in 1935. That year he became first secretary of the Moscow city and regional party organization. The next year, at the 17th Party Congress, he was elected a full member of the 70-man Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union. Khrushchev was a zealous supporter of Stalin in those years and participated in the purges of party leadership. He was one of only 3 provincial secretaries who survived the mass executions of the Great Purge of the 1930s. In 1936 he became a member of the Constitutional Committee and in 1937 he became an alternate member of the Central Committee’s ruling Politburo and a member of the Foreign Affairs Commission of the Supreme Soviet. A year later Khrushchev was made a candidate member of the Politburo and sent to Kiev as first secretary of the Ukrainian party organization, and made a full member of the Politburo in 1939. He was tasked with integrating Poland into the USSR during WWII. He was attached to the Soviet army with the rank of Lieutenant General in order to evacuate as much of Ukraine’s industry as possible to the east as well as stimulate the resistance of the civilian population. He later re-assumed control of Ukraine as first secretary of the Ukrainian party organization. After Stalin’s death in 1953, Khrushchev engaged in a power struggle with Malenkov, who was Stalin’s heir apparent. Khrushchev achieved control and later went on to contribute many reforms which changed the Russian society and their relations with other nations. Khrushchev was forced to retire by a coup and lived the rest of his days in obscurity. He would occasionally be seen in public to vote in elections. Khrushchev died on September 11, 1971.
